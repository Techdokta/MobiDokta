Is the iPhone 16 More Repairable? A Workshop Engineer's Take

By MobiDokta Engineering Team  ·  29 June 2026  ·  7 min read

Back to Blog

Every time Apple releases a new iPhone, the same question lands in our workshop: "Is it easier to repair than the last one?" For the iPhone 16, the honest answer is — it depends on what you're fixing. There are genuine improvements that make our job easier in specific areas, and there are software-level restrictions that create new friction. Here's what we've actually seen on the bench in Pretoria.

The Battery Adhesive Change Is Real — And It Matters

The most significant engineering change for repairability on the iPhone 16 is the battery mounting system. Previous models — from the iPhone 12 through 15 — used pull-tab adhesive strips that required a careful, specific technique to remove without tearing. When those tabs snapped (and they do snap, especially on older devices where the adhesive had hardened over time), you were looking at a 20-minute isopropyl alcohol soak to loosen the battery before it could safely come out.

Apple switched to a low-voltage electrolytic adhesive on the iPhone 16. A small voltage applied through the logic board connector causes the adhesive to release — the battery essentially pops free without mechanical prying or heat. In practice, this reduces the risk of puncturing the cell during removal, which is a real safety concern. A punctured lithium-ion battery can thermal-runaway inside a workshop, and in a Highveld summer, heat is already a factor we manage carefully. This change is a genuine win for safety and speed.

iFixit awarded the iPhone 16 an 8 out of 10 repairability score — one of the highest scores ever given to an iPhone — and the battery adhesive change is a major reason for that rating. We'd largely agree, with some important caveats below.

Parts Pairing: The Software Wall

Here's where the complexity sits. Apple's parts pairing system means that screens, batteries, cameras, and Face ID components are all cryptographically linked to the device's serial number in Apple's servers. Fitting an aftermarket or donor screen to an iPhone 16 without calibration will still work for basic touch and display — but you'll lose True Tone colour calibration, and the system will flag the part in Settings under Battery Health and Display, showing a "genuine Apple part could not be verified" warning.

For independent repair shops across South Africa, this creates a real-world limitation. We can physically replace the screen or battery without any problem — the mechanical side is fine. But restoring full software calibration requires either Apple's proprietary GSX system (available to Apple Authorised Service Providers) or third-party tools that mirror the calibration process. At MobiDokta, we work with calibration-capable tooling, which means you won't sit with persistent warning messages or degraded functionality after your repair. That said, customers should know this is not a simple one-size-fits-all situation — and shops that don't address it leave your phone in a partially repaired state.

We always recommend getting a repair done by a workshop that understands the parts pairing requirements. A screen that costs R500 less but leaves you with a permanent warning notification and no True Tone is not a saving — it's a compromise you'll notice every day.

A Note on Screen Protectors and UV Glue

One thing worth mentioning: if you're planning to apply a screen protector to your iPhone 16, avoid the type that uses UV-curing adhesive to bond to the lens. This type of protector can trap the adhesive against the oleophobic (fingerprint-resistant) coating on the screen and damage it permanently when removed. We see this every few weeks in the workshop. Stick to tempered glass protectors with a standard silicone adhesive backing — they work just as well and don't interact badly with the display coating.

Bottom Line

The iPhone 16 is meaningfully more repairable at the mechanical level than any iPhone before it. The electrolytic battery adhesive is a real improvement. But Apple's software pairing ecosystem means you still need a workshop with proper calibration capability, not just a technician who can swap a screen.
