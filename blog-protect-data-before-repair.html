Is Your Data Safe at a Repair Shop? Here's How to Make Sure

By MobiDokta Engineering Team  ·  29 June 2026  ·  7 min read

Back to Blog

Data privacy is the question we get asked more than almost any other. And it's a completely reasonable concern — your phone holds your banking apps, your messages, your photos, your work emails, and information about your daily life that you'd never hand to a stranger. Walking into a repair shop and leaving your unlocked device with someone you've just met requires a level of trust that deserves an honest, detailed explanation. Here's how we think about it, and what you can do to protect yourself regardless of which shop you use.

What Type of Repair You're Having Changes Everything

The first thing to understand is that not all repairs are equal from a data perspective. For the two most common repairs — screen replacement and battery replacement — your data is never at risk in any meaningful sense. These repairs require the technician to open the device, remove and replace the physical component, and close it again. The logic board (the part of the phone that actually stores and processes your data) is never touched, never removed, and never connected to anything. A screen replacement is no different, from a data perspective, to changing a tyre on your car.

Where the picture changes slightly is with board-level repairs — water damage diagnostics, micro-soldering work, or any repair where the phone needs to power on for testing. Here, the technician will see your lock screen and potentially your home screen during functional tests. At MobiDokta, our engineers are trained to power on the device only to the point needed for the diagnostic — we're checking whether the touch screen responds, whether the cameras activate, whether the charging circuit is working. We are not opening apps, browsing your photos, or accessing any personal content. This isn't just a policy — it's logged. Every repair is tied to a device serial number and a timestamped work order.

What to Do Before Bringing In Your Phone

Even if you trust your repair shop completely, a backup before any repair is simply good practice. Phones have been dropped on workshop floors. Parts occasionally have hidden faults. Unexpected data loss during a repair, while rare, does happen in this industry. Here's the standard pre-repair checklist we recommend:

  • iPhone — iCloud Backup: Go to Settings > [Your Name] > iCloud > iCloud Backup > Back Up Now. Wait for it to complete. You'll see the date and time of the last backup at the bottom of that screen — confirm it shows today's date before you leave the house.
  • Android — Google Backup: Go to Settings > Google > Backup > Back Up Now. This covers contacts, app data, call history, SMS messages, and device settings. Note that Google Photos needs to sync separately — open the app and confirm it shows "Backup is on" and is up to date.
  • WhatsApp: WhatsApp has its own backup that runs separately from your phone's main backup. Open WhatsApp > Settings > Chats > Chat Backup > Back Up Now. This is especially important — WhatsApp data is not included in standard Google or iCloud backups by default on most configurations.
  • Screenshot your home screen layout. App restore doesn't always recreate your home screen organisation exactly. One screenshot takes two seconds and saves you ten minutes of rearranging.
  • Note any unsaved items: voice memos, recent photos taken before your last backup synced, or anything that only exists locally on the device.

What You Don't Need to Do

We regularly have customers come in who have factory reset their phones before a screen repair because they were worried about their data. This is unnecessary for screen and battery repairs and means you've already lost your local data before the repair even starts. Do not factory reset your phone before a standard hardware repair — back it up instead. The backup puts you in control of a restore; a factory reset just destroys everything upfront.

Similarly, you don't need to change your PIN or remove your SIM card for a screen or battery repair. The device doesn't need to be unlocked for these repairs. If a shop asks you to provide your PIN for a screen replacement, that's worth questioning.

Find My iPhone and Google Account Lock

If you're bringing in a device for a repair that requires powering it on and off (software flashing, certain motherboard repairs), some shops will ask you to temporarily disable Find My iPhone or Google Account lock. This is because these security systems prevent the device from being restored or activated by a third party without your credentials. For routine repairs at MobiDokta, we don't ask for this — but if your specific repair does require it, we'll explain why in writing before proceeding. You should always keep your Apple ID and password handy in case it's needed during a repair involving a software restore.

MobiDokta's POPIA Compliance and Data Handling Policy

South Africa's Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) applies to any operator that processes personal information — and a device handed in for repair falls within that scope. At MobiDokta, our engineers sign a data handling agreement as part of their employment. We do not access personal content on customer devices, we do not copy or transfer data off devices, and all repairs are logged with the device's serial number, the technician ID, and a timestamped work record. Our intake process includes a written acknowledgement that the device is handed in for a specific repair and that personal data remains the property and responsibility of the customer.

When choosing any repair shop in South Africa, here's a simple checklist to verify their data stance: ask whether they have a POPIA compliance statement, check their Google reviews for any mentions of data concerns, and note whether they issue a written repair receipt that describes the specific work being done. A shop that can't provide a written job card is a shop that has no accountability trail if something goes wrong.

The Short Version

For screen and battery repairs: back up your phone, bring it in, and relax — your data is on the logic board which doesn't get touched. For more complex repairs: make sure your backup is current, know your Apple ID or Google credentials, and choose a shop with a clear, written data policy.
